**Rationale**: This was a high-quality debate between two well-prepared analysts, and both sides landed genuine blows. The 3.3x share dilution in two years (90M to 298M shares) is a real cost to existing shareholders that the bull repeatedly sidestepped.

On technicals, the bear was more disciplined. The price at $106 is 19.5% above the 10 EMA, above the upper Bollinger Band, and the VWMA at $81 vs. price creates a $25 vacuum. The volume declining from 39.7M to 30.6M on the breakout high is a textbook negative divergence. The RSI at 68 puts the stock at the doorstep of overbought. These are not imaginary signals — they are statistical realities that have historically resolved through mean reversion.

The bull's competition argument was weaker than the bear let on, but the bear's counter was stronger: SpaceX has thousands of satellites, T-Mobile, reusable rockets, and the capacity to iterate fast. Carriers are demonstrably multi-sourcing. ASTS's "lock-in" is more aspirational than contractual.

The bear's historical framing — comparing ASTS not to Amazon or Netflix but to Iridium, Globalstar, Nikola, and WeWork — landed hardest. Companies with massive TAM stories, big-name partnerships, cash piles, and working prototypes have gone to zero before. The bull never adequately addressed why ASTS at 484x sales avoids that fate.

That said, a full Sell overreaches. The technology is real. The carrier JV is meaningful even if it's not booked as revenue. The SpaceX IPO genuinely lifts the sector. A short squeeze could punish shorts in the near term. The bull's core strategic thesis — that ASTS is building critical infrastructure for a massive market — is not wrong; it's just that the current price embeds a margin of error that is dangerously thin.

The recommendation is Underweight: the bear arguments carry the debate on valuation, risk/reward, and technical setup, but the genuine strategic merits and near-term squeeze risk prevent a full exit.
