**Recommendation**: Underweight

**Rationale**: The debate between the Bull and Bear analysts on RCI.B was rigorous and illuminating. Both sides surfaced real data, but the Bear carried the stronger overall argument — not by fear-mongering, but by exposing structural weaknesses the Bull could not fully refute.

The Bull's strongest cards: forward P/E of 10.41x, PEG of 0.86 on consensus growth, a 3.81% dividend with a 44% payout ratio from FCF, the Shaw acquisition synergies ($1B+ over 3 years), an 8-year network leadership streak, and a technical setup that *could* flip to a golden cross within weeks. These are not trivial points, and they provide a credible floor under the stock.

But the Bear won the debate where it mattered most — free cash flow sustainability and balance-sheet reality. The Bull's own numbers confirmed that FCF after interest and dividends is *negative ~$779M*. The Bull framed this as "by design" — prioritizing debt paydown — but the Bear correctly identified it as a structural cash-flow gap that leaves zero margin for error. A recession, a rate shock, or a competitive price war from Quebecor's Freedom Mobile would expose that fragility immediately.

The Bear also successfully challenged the valuation premium: at 10.4x forward earnings, Rogers trades above BCE (9.8x) and Telus (9.5x), both of which carry lower debt/EBITDA and positive tangible equity. The 12% revenue CAGR is almost entirely acquisition-driven; organic growth is 2–5%, which makes the PEG on real organic growth closer to 3x — not cheap. The "valuation gift" narrative doesn't survive peer-relative scrutiny.

On technicals, the Bear was more disciplined: the death cross is still in effect (50-SMA at $49.66 vs. 200-SMA at $50.60), MACD histogram has compressed 67%, the RSI trend is down from overbought, and price has failed three times at $53–$55 resistance. The Bull's golden-cross forecast is plausible but probabilistic, not inevitable — and the weight of the current technical evidence leans bearish.

The Bear's macro argument also held up better: Rogers is down 5% YTD while Canadian utilities are up 4%. That's not defensive behavior — it's underperformance being priced in. Elevated VIX, sticky inflation, and refinancing risk on $44.8B of debt are genuine headwinds, not transient noise.

That said, the Bear's "Sell/Avoid" call overreaches. The dividend at 3.81% is genuinely covered from operating cash flow before debt service choices. The Shaw integration is real and will improve EBITDA. The network leadership is not just a marketing trophy — it's an independently audited advantage. And the golden cross, while not certain, is a near-term catalyst that could shift sentiment quickly. A full exit risks missing a re-rate if any one of these tailwinds materializes.

The right posture is caution without outright exit: trim exposure, don't add new capital, and wait for either the golden-cross confirmation or a pullback to the $47–$49 range where the risk/reward would be genuinely attractive. The current $52.50 level offers asymmetric downside — 9% to the lower Bollinger band vs. 5% to resistance — and the probability-weighted expected value tilts negative based on realistic outcome probabilities.
